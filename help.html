How to Use QuantumGuard — Step by Step

1
Open the Home page
Go to localhost:8080 in your browser after starting the Java server.
2
Select your sector
Choose Healthcare, Banking, Government, Education, or Other. This adjusts how seriously each finding is scored. Healthcare and Government findings are weighted 1.3× higher because that data is more sensitive.
3
Paste code or upload a file
Paste any source code into the text area, or upload a file (.java .js .ts .py .php .c .cpp .cs .go .rb .kt .swift .rs). Max size: 50KB. One file at a time. Or click a sample button to load pre-written vulnerable code instantly.
4
Click Scan Now
The Java backend scans every line against 15 CVE rules using regex matching. Results appear in under 1 second for most files. The SHA-3-256 hash of your code is shown — proof of quantum-safe verification.
5
Read your grade on the Results page
You get a letter grade A to F. A = very secure. F = critical issues found. The score is calculated as: 100 minus the sum of severity weights (15 for Critical, 10 for High, 5 for Warning) multiplied by your sector multiplier.
6
Read each finding
Each finding shows the exact line number, the CVE ID, the present-day risk, and the Quantum Impact — how quantum computers will make this worse by 2030. Use the filter buttons (Critical / High / Warning) to focus. Click Dismiss for false positives.
7
Use the Retrofit Checklist tab
Switch to the Retrofit Checklist tab on the Results page. Each finding shows the exact insecure code and the exact secure replacement for your language. Tick off fixes as you complete them.
8
Visit the Dashboard
See your score, crypto agility score, bar chart of issues by category, and the quantum threat timeline showing when each algorithm is expected to be broken. Use the HNDL Risk Calculator to calculate your specific data risk.
9
Visit Quantum Education
See Shor's and Grover's algorithm educational visualisers. Understand why RSA will be broken and why MD5 is already broken. Watch the Live Attack Monitor to see QuantumGuard defending itself in real time.
10
Download your PDF Report
Go to the Report page and click Download PDF. Your report includes every finding, the exact fix, NIST FIPS references, India NQM 2027 alignment, and your overall grade. Share it with your team, professor, or auditor.

Plain English Glossary

A numbered record of a known security vulnerability — like a bug report for the whole world. Example: CVE-2004-2761 is the official record of MD5 being broken. Every CVE has an ID, a description, and a severity score. QuantumGuard maps every finding to a real CVE from the National Vulnerability Database (nvd.nist.gov).

Encryption algorithms that are safe against both classical computers AND quantum computers. NIST finalised 3 PQC standards in August 2024: CRYSTALS-Kyber (FIPS 203) for key exchange, CRYSTALS-Dilithium (FIPS 204) for signatures, and SHA-3 (FIPS 202) for hashing. QuantumGuard runs SHA-3 and CRYSTALS-Kyber on its own server via Bouncy Castle.

A quantum-resistant hashing algorithm standardised by NIST (FIPS 202). Unlike MD5 and SHA-1 which use Merkle-Damgård construction (now broken), SHA-3 uses the Keccak sponge function. Even with Grover's quantum algorithm, breaking SHA-3-256 requires 2^128 operations — infeasible forever. QuantumGuard hashes every uploaded file with SHA-3.

The NIST FIPS 203 standard that replaces RSA for key exchange. Based on Module Learning With Errors (MLWE) — a mathematical problem no quantum algorithm can efficiently solve. Shor's algorithm only breaks number-theory problems (RSA, ECC) — it has no effect on MLWE. QuantumGuard uses Kyber to encrypt stored scan results.

A quantum algorithm that can factor large numbers (the basis of RSA) in polynomial time O(log³N) using quantum superposition and Quantum Fourier Transform. A classical computer takes longer than the age of the universe to factor RSA-2048. A quantum computer running Shor's algorithm can do it in approximately 8 hours (Gidney and Ekerå, 2021). This makes all RSA and ECC encryption breakable by 2030.

A quantum search algorithm that finds an item in an unsorted list of N items in O(√N) steps instead of O(N) classically. Applied to encryption: it halves the effective key length. AES-128 becomes effectively AES-64 (classically breakable). MD5 cracking drops from seconds to microseconds. This is why QuantumGuard recommends AES-256 (still safe: √(2^256) = 2^128 — infeasible).

A measure of how easily your code can switch to new cryptographic algorithms. High agility (9/10) means all crypto calls are in one or two places — easy to update. Low agility (1/10) means crypto is scattered across hundreds of lines — very expensive to migrate. QuantumGuard calculates this as: unique crypto lines / total lines. The lower the concentration, the higher the agility score.

An attack strategy where adversaries record encrypted data today and store it, waiting for quantum computers to arrive so they can decrypt it later. This attack is happening RIGHT NOW — documented by NSA, GCHQ, and CERT-In. Your health records, financial transactions, and government data encrypted today with RSA or SHA-1 may be readable by 2030. The HNDL Risk Calculator shows your specific exposure.

The US government agency that sets global cryptography standards. In August 2024 NIST finalised three post-quantum cryptography standards: FIPS 202 (SHA-3), FIPS 203 (CRYSTALS-Kyber), FIPS 204 (CRYSTALS-Dilithium), and FIPS 205 (SPHINCS+). Every finding in QuantumGuard maps to a specific NIST standard.
